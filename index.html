Core Investing Mental Models

These mental models form the intellectual toolkit of the world's greatest investors. Each one transforms how you evaluate opportunities.

🛡
Value Investing

Margin of Safety

The most important concept in investing. Buy assets at a significant discount to their intrinsic value. The margin between price and value protects you from errors in analysis, bad luck, and market downturns.

"The function of the margin of safety is, in essence, that of rendering unnecessary an accurate estimate of the future." - Benjamin Graham
Application: If you calculate a company is worth $100/share, only buy at $65-70 (30-35% margin). This cushion absorbs estimation errors. The larger your uncertainty, the larger the margin you need.

Value Investing

Circle of Competence

Know what you know, and more importantly, know what you don't know. Invest only in areas where you have genuine understanding. The size of your circle matters less than knowing its boundaries.

"What an investor needs is the ability to correctly evaluate selected businesses. Note that word 'selected': You don't have to be an expert on every company. You only have to be able to evaluate companies within your circle of competence." - Warren Buffett
  • Identify industries you genuinely understand
  • Be honest about the edges of your knowledge
  • Slowly expand your circle through deep study
  • Say "I don't know" more often - it's a strength
🎭
Behavioral

Mr. Market

Benjamin Graham's brilliant allegory: imagine the stock market as a manic-depressive business partner who offers to buy or sell shares every day. Some days he's euphoric (prices too high), other days despondent (prices too low).

"Mr. Market is there to serve you, not to guide you. It is his pocketbook, not his wisdom, that you will find useful." - Benjamin Graham
Key Insight: You don't have to trade with Mr. Market every day. Wait for his irrational moments: buy when he's depressed and selling cheap, sell (or avoid) when he's euphoric and overpricing everything. Patience is the edge. See more at KeepRule Scenarios.

Growth

Compounding

Einstein allegedly called compound interest the eighth wonder of the world. The key insight: small consistent returns over long periods produce extraordinary results. Time is the investor's greatest ally.

$10,000 invested at 10% annuallyValue
After 10 years$25,937
After 20 years$67,275
After 30 years$174,494
After 40 years$452,593

The last decade produces more absolute returns than the first three combined. This is why starting early matters enormously.

Risk Management

Inversion

Instead of asking "How do I succeed?", ask "How would I guarantee failure?" Then avoid those things. Charlie Munger's favorite mental model: "All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there."

"It is remarkable how much long-term advantage people like us have gotten by trying to be consistently not stupid, instead of trying to be very intelligent." - Charlie Munger
Inversion in Investing: How to guarantee portfolio destruction: Use excessive leverage, concentrate in one sector, trade on emotion, ignore valuations, follow the crowd. Therefore: use moderate leverage (or none), diversify, be systematic, respect value, think independently. Read more from the masters at KeepRule.

Value Investing

Economic Moat

Coined by Warren Buffett, an economic moat is a durable competitive advantage that protects a company's profits from competition. Wide moats allow companies to earn above-average returns for decades.

  • Network Effects: The product becomes more valuable as more people use it (Visa, Facebook)
  • Switching Costs: Customers face high costs to switch (Enterprise software, banking)
  • Cost Advantages: Structural cost advantages competitors can't replicate (Costco, GEICO)
  • Intangible Assets: Brands, patents, regulatory licenses (Disney, pharmaceutical patents)
  • Efficient Scale: Market only supports one or few profitable players (Railroads, utilities)

Risk Management

Asymmetric Risk/Reward

The best investments have limited downside but enormous upside. Instead of asking "what's the expected return?", ask "what's the worst case, and can I survive it?"

Example: Venture-Style Thinking Invest $10K in 10 startups. 7 fail completely (-$7K). 2 return 3x ($6K). 1 returns 20x ($20K). Total: $19K return on $10K investment despite 70% failure rate. The key is sizing positions to survive the losses.

Behavioral

Second-Order Thinking

First-order thinking: "This event is positive for the company." Second-order thinking: "This event is positive, but everyone already expects it, so it's priced in. What happens next that no one is considering?"

The Trap of Consensus: If everyone agrees a stock is great, that optimism is already reflected in the price. Profits come from correctly anticipating what others haven't yet considered. Explore contrarian principles at KeepRule.
Value Investing

Owner's Mentality

Don't think of stocks as ticker symbols. Think of them as partial ownership of real businesses. Would you buy this entire company at the current market cap? If not, why buy a small piece?

"If you aren't willing to own a stock for ten years, don't even think about owning it for ten minutes." - Warren Buffett

This mental model eliminates short-term noise and focuses on business fundamentals.

Risk Management

Antifragility

From Nassim Taleb: build portfolios that don't just survive volatility but actually benefit from it. Position sizing, optionality, and convex payoffs are the keys.

  • Keep cash reserves to buy during crashes (benefit from disorder)
  • Use small positions in high-upside opportunities (convex payoffs)
  • Avoid fragile positions: excessive leverage, concentration, illiquidity
  • Let time work for you, not against you (avoid time decay)
Behavioral

Incentive Analysis

"Show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome." - Charlie Munger. Before investing, ask: How is management compensated? How do analysts make money? Whose interests are aligned with yours?

Red Flags: - Management compensated by revenue, not profit (empire building)
- Stock buybacks funded by debt (financial engineering)
- Aggressive accounting that flatters earnings
- Related-party transactions that benefit insiders

Growth

Opportunity Cost

Every investment decision is simultaneously a decision NOT to invest that capital elsewhere. The true cost of any investment is the best alternative you gave up.

"The wise ones bet heavily when the world offers them that opportunity. They bet big when they have the odds. And the rest of the time, they don't." - Charlie Munger

This means: hold cash when opportunities are poor, concentrate when they're exceptional. Explore opportunity cost thinking at KeepRule Blog.

Applying Mental Models: A Practical Workflow

Use this step-by-step process to apply mental models to any investment decision.

The Investment Analysis Checklist

  • Circle of Competence Check: Do I genuinely understand this business, its customers, and its competitive dynamics?
  • Moat Analysis: What protects this business from competition? Is the moat widening or narrowing?
  • Inversion: How could this investment go to zero? What would cause permanent capital loss?
  • Margin of Safety: Am I paying at least 25-30% below my estimate of intrinsic value?
  • Incentive Check: Is management aligned with shareholders? How are they compensated?
  • Second-Order Effects: What does the market already expect? What am I seeing that others are missing?
  • Opportunity Cost: Is this the best use of my capital right now compared to all alternatives?
  • Position Sizing: Even if I am right, how much should I risk? What does Kelly Criterion suggest?

Common Mental Model Mistakes

Even experienced investors misapply mental models. Watch out for these traps:

Confirmation Bias in Moat Analysis: Seeing moats where none exist because you already like the company. Always look for evidence AGAINST your thesis first.
False Margin of Safety: Using optimistic intrinsic value estimates to justify a purchase. Your margin of safety is only as good as your valuation is conservative.
Ignoring Opportunity Cost: Holding mediocre positions because they are not losing money, when better opportunities exist elsewhere. Capital has a time value.
